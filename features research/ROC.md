# Rate of Change
Commonly used in finance and economics, a ROC is the speed at which a metric, ratio, or variable changes over a specified period

ROC is often used by analysts when discussing momentum, and it can generally be expressed as a ratio between a change in one variable relative to a corresponding change in another

## Understanding ROC
The ROC is used to mathematically describe the percentage change in value over a defined period of time, representing the momentum of a variable

> It can also be used in finance and investing to describe the change in the value of an asset or index over time.

The ROC is an important financial concept because it allows investors to spot security momentum and other trends
- for example, a security with high momentum, or one that has a +ve ROC, normally outperforms the market in the short term
	- conversely, a security is more likely to decline if it has an ROC that falls below its MA or one that has a low or -ve ROC
	- in this case, the ROC could be seen as a sell signal to investors

The ROC is also a good indicator of market bubbles
- even though momentum is good and traders look for securities with a +ve ROC, if a broad-market ETF, index, or mutual fund has a sharp increase in its ROC in the short term, it may be a sign that the market is unstable

Traders can also pay close attention to the speed at which one price changes relative to another
- for example, options traders study the relationship between the ROC in the price of an option relative to a small change in the price of the underlying asset, known as option's delta

## How to Find the ROC
The general formula for the ROC is:
$$ROC = \frac{X_{1} - X_{2}}{T_{1 - T_{2}}}$$
- where $(X_{1} - X_{2})$ is the change in the variable being measured
- and $(T_{1} - T_{2})$ is the amount of time it took for the change to happen

This formula can also be written as:
$$
R = \frac{D_{2} - D_{1}}{T}
$$
- where:
	- $R$ = ROC
	- $D$ = distance (or some other variable) measured at the beginning and end of the period
	- $T$ = the time it took for that change to occur

In finance, the calculation for ROC can also be computed as a return over time
- in this instance, the formula uses the current value of a stock or index and divides it by the value from an earlier period
- subtract 1 and multiply the resulting number by 100 to give it a percentage representation
$$
ROC = (\frac{\text{current value}}{\text{previous value}} -1) * 100
$$

## The Price ROC Indicator
The ROC is most often used to measure the change in a security's price over time
- this is known as the Price ROC
- the Price ROC can be derived by taking the price of a security at time $B$ minus the price of the same security at time $A$ and dividing that result by the price at time $A$
$$
\begin{align}
&\text{Price ROC} = \frac{B-A}{A} \times 100 \\ \\
&where: \\
 & B = \text{price at current time} \\
 & A = \text{price at previous time}
\end{align}
$$

When it is +ve, prices are accelerating upward
When it is -ve, price acceleration is downward

## How ROC is Used
Used in technical analysis to measure momentum

A +ve ROC can confirm a bullish trend, while a -ve ROC indicates a bearish one
- when the price is consolidating, the ROC will hover near zero
